# Compare Studies

## When to Use
Use this skill when you have two or more research studies addressing similar questions and need to understand how their findings relate to each other. Activate when someone asks "how do these studies compare?" or "do these papers agree?" or "what does the literature say about X?" This skill is essential during literature review phases when synthesizing evidence from multiple sources. It is distinct from summarizing a single paper — this skill focuses on cross-study comparison and synthesis rather than individual paper extraction.

## Steps
1. **Establish the comparison framework**: Define the dimensions along which the studies will be compared — research question, methodology, sample characteristics, findings, and conclusions.
2. **Summarize each study briefly**: For each study, create a one-paragraph summary covering the research question, method, and main finding. Use consistent terminology across summaries.
3. **Compare research questions**: Assess whether the studies address the exact same question or related but distinct questions. Note any differences in scope, population, or outcome measures.
4. **Compare methodologies**: Create a side-by-side comparison of research design, sample size, data collection methods, time period, and analytical approaches. Highlight methodological strengths and weaknesses of each.
5. **Compare findings**: Identify where findings agree (consensus), where they disagree (contradictions), and where one study covers ground the others don't (gaps).
6. **Analyze contradictions**: For conflicting findings, explore potential explanations — differences in methodology, sample, time period, geographic context, or statistical approach.
7. **Assess relative evidence strength**: Rank the studies by evidence quality considering design rigor, sample size, and potential biases. Note which findings carry more weight.
8. **Synthesize conclusions**: Write an overall synthesis that integrates the evidence from all studies into a coherent narrative about what is known, what is uncertain, and what remains unanswered.
9. **Identify research gaps**: Based on the comparison, note specific questions or methodological approaches that future research should address.

## Output Format
A structured comparison report with sections: Studies Included (citation list), Comparison Matrix (tabular side-by-side of key dimensions), Areas of Consensus, Areas of Contradiction (with possible explanations), Evidence Strength Assessment, Integrated Synthesis, and Identified Gaps. Include the comparison matrix as a markdown table.

## Common Pitfalls
- Treating all studies as equally credible without assessing methodology quality and potential biases.
- Comparing studies with fundamentally different research questions as if they should reach the same conclusion.
- Focusing only on whether findings "agree or disagree" without examining why differences exist.
- Not considering publication bias — the studies that got published may not represent the full evidence landscape.
